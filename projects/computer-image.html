As a research assistant for an upcoming exhibition, I compiled a database of file formats for 3d modeling and architectural design. I also created a series of data visualizations to elucidate their structures — digital architectures of geometric data types.

This work was conducted under the supervision of Professor Daniel Cardoso Llach of Carnegie Mellon University School of Architecture's Computational Design program.

Introductory Research

File formats negotiate between the desires of architectural designers and the practicalities of computer programmers and the machines and software that interpret the files.

I started the research by reading the specifications for the DXF and IGES file formats, an interchange format and industry standard, respectively. I focused on the overall structure of an individual file, which typically have a header section that defines metadata (i.e. software used to generate it, author) and a body that houses the geometric data. Between the two formats, IGES offers more types of entities — recognized data structures that translate to a specific type of geometry. Another standard, the IFC format, has even more data types that correspond to real-world objects, such as doors and windows.

I graphed some these file formats so far, as well as the OBJ format, with separate axes for number of geometric entities and number of real-world entities afforded.

To help with my understanding of file formats in general, I loosely defined three "classes" of formats:

1. Granular Representation

  • ex. OBJ, STL
  • small set of geometric primitives
  • form develops from accumulation of "grains" of data
  • high interoperability (easy to process/render)

2. Parametric Representation

  • ex. DXF, 3DM, 3DS
  • large set of geometric primitives (abstract) and/or ability to describe arbitrary, complex, irregular forms succinctly/parametrically
  • can describe same form in multiple ways
  • medium interoperability

3. Contextual Representation

  • ex. IGES, IFC, RVT
  • situates parametric representation in a particular context (site, materials, energy, etc.)
  • may blend parametric and granular representations (form secondary to context)
  • low interoperability, may be software-specific
